# 🟦 UNIT – I: BASIC CONCEPTS OF TECHNOLOGY AND LAW

## 📌 1.1 Internet Technology
├── **Definition:** The Internet is a global network of interconnected computers using **TCP/IP** protocols to exchange information.
├── **Key Points:**
- Origin: ARPANET (1969) → modern Internet (1990s)
- Uses **client-server** and **peer-to-peer** models
- Key protocols: TCP/IP, HTTP, HTTPS, FTP, SMTP, DNS
- World Wide Web (WWW) is built on top of internet
- Access: ISP → Backbone → Servers worldwide
- Key terms: IP address, DNS, URL, Web Browser, ISP
├── **One-line Exam Answer:** The Internet is a global network of computers using TCP/IP protocols, providing services like email, WWW, file transfer, and online communication.

## 📌 1.5 Cybercrimes — Categories under IT Act

### A. Tampering with Computer Source Documents (Section 65)
- Concealing, destroying, altering source code
- **Punishment:** Imprisonment up to 3 years AND/OR fine up to ₹2 lakh
- Source code = listing, design, programs, layouts

### B. Hacking with Computer System (Section 66)
- Originally Section 66 specifically defined hacking
- Now covered under broader Section 43 + 66
- **Punishment:** Imprisonment up to 3 years AND/OR fine up to ₹5 lakh

### C. Publishing Obscene Information in Electronic Form (Section 67)
- Publishing/transmitting obscene material via electronic means
- **Punishment:** Up to 3 years + ₹5 lakh (1st conviction); up to 5 years + ₹10 lakh (subsequent)

### D. Breach of Confidentiality & Privacy (Section 72)
- Unauthorized disclosure of accessed information
- **Punishment:** Up to 2 years OR fine up to ₹1 lakh

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** IT Act 2000 categorizes cybercrimes including tampering source code (Sec 65), hacking (Sec 66), publishing obscene material (Sec 67), and breach of confidentiality (Sec 72).

# 🟩 UNIT – II: LAW OF DIGITAL CONTRACTS

## 📌 2.1 Essence of Digital Contracts
├── **Definition:** A Digital Contract (or e-contract) is a legally enforceable agreement created and executed electronically, without paper.
├── **Key Points:**
- Recognized under **Section 10A of IT Act 2000**
- Must satisfy basic contract requirements: offer, acceptance, consideration, free consent, lawful object
- **Types:**
  1. **Click-wrap** – click "I Agree"
  2. **Browse-wrap** – continued use implies acceptance
  3. **Shrink-wrap** – terms inside packaging
  4. **E-mail contracts**
- Parties must intend to be legally bound
- **Authentication:** Digital signature or e-signature

├── **Examples:** Online shopping (Amazon), Software licenses, SaaS terms.

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** A Digital Contract is a legally binding agreement formed electronically, governed by Section 10A of IT Act 2000.

---

## 📌 2.2 Digital Signatures
├── **Definition:** A Digital Signature is a cryptographic technique that authenticates electronic documents and verifies the sender's identity.
├── **How it Works:**
1. Sender creates **hash** of document
2. **Encrypts hash with private key** = digital signature
3. Sends document + signature
4. Receiver decrypts signature with **sender's public key**
5. Compares with own computed hash
6. Match → document is authentic

├── **Legal Basis:** Section 3 of IT Act 2000 — digital signatures legally valid.
├── **Provides:** Authentication, Integrity, Non-repudiation
├── **One-line Exam Answer:** A Digital Signature is a cryptographic mechanism providing authentication, integrity, and non-repudiation for electronic documents, legally valid under IT Act Section 3.

---

## 📌 2.3 Certifying Authorities (CA)
├── **Definition:** A Certifying Authority is a trusted entity authorized to issue **Digital Signature Certificates (DSC)**.
├── **Functions:**
- Issue digital certificates
- Verify identity (KYC)
- Maintain certificate database
- Suspend/revoke certificates
- Publish CRL (Certificate Revocation List)
├── **Regulatory body in India:** **Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA)**
├── **Licensed CAs:** eMudhra, Sify, NIC, IDRBT, Capricorn, NSDL
├── **DSC Classes:**
- **Class 1** – email verification only
- **Class 2** – identity verification (now discontinued)
- **Class 3** – physical verification (mandatory for e-tendering)
├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Certifying Authorities issue digital signature certificates and are regulated by the Controller of Certifying Authorities (CCA) under IT Act 2000.

---

## 📌 2.4 Cryptography
├── **Definition:** Cryptography is the science of securing information by converting plaintext to ciphertext using mathematical algorithms.
├── **Goals (CIA + N):** Confidentiality, Integrity, Authentication, Non-repudiation

### Types:
| Type | Key | Algorithms |
|------|-----|------------|
| **Symmetric** | Same key | DES, AES, 3DES |
| **Asymmetric** | Public + Private | RSA, ECC, DSA |
| **Hash Functions** | No key | MD5, SHA-256, SHA-3 |

### Common Algorithms:
- **AES** – Advanced Encryption Standard (block cipher, 128/192/256-bit)
- **RSA** – Rivest-Shamir-Adleman (public-key)
- **SHA-256** – cryptographic hash (used in Bitcoin)
- **Diffie-Hellman** – key exchange

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Cryptography is the science of secure communication using symmetric, asymmetric, or hash algorithms to ensure confidentiality, integrity, and authentication.

## 📌 2.5 Intellectual Property Rights (IPR)
├── **Definition:** Legal rights granted to creators over their inventions, literary works, designs, symbols, and creations of the mind.

### Types of IPR:
1. **Copyright** – literary, artistic, musical, software (60+ years lifetime)
2. **Patent** – inventions (20 years)
3. **Trademark** – brand names, logos (10 years renewable)
4. **Industrial Design** – appearance of products (10+5 years)
5. **Geographical Indication (GI)** – Darjeeling tea, Kashmir saffron
6. **Trade Secret** – confidential business info
7. **Plant Variety Protection**

├── **Why IPR matters in Cyber Space:**
- Software protection
- Online content (music, video)
- Domain names
- Database protection
- Online brand protection

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** IPR are legal rights granted to creators for their inventions and creative works — including copyright, patent, trademark, design, and GI.

---

## 📌 2.6 Patent Law
├── **Definition:** Patent is an exclusive right granted for an **invention** that is new, useful, and non-obvious.
├── **Indian Law:** Patents Act 1970 (amended)
├── **Criteria:**
  1. Novelty
  2. Inventive Step (non-obviousness)
  3. Industrial Application (utility)
├── **Duration:** 20 years from filing
├── **NOT Patentable in India (Sec 3):**
- Software per se (Sec 3(k))
- Mathematical/business methods
- Mere discovery
- Plants/animals
├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Patent Law (Patents Act 1970) grants 20-year exclusive rights for novel, useful, and non-obvious inventions; software per se is not patentable in India.

---

## 📌 2.7 Copyright Law
├── **Definition:** Copyright is the legal right granted to creators of original literary, artistic, musical, dramatic works.
├── **Indian Law:** Copyright Act 1957
├── **Duration:** Author's lifetime + **60 years** (in India)
├── **Protects expression**, not idea
├── **Software** is protected as a **literary work**
├── **Rights:**
- Reproduction, distribution, adaptation, performance, communication
├── **Infringement Punishment (Sec 63):** 6 months to 3 years imprisonment + fine ₹50,000 to ₹2 lakh
├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Copyright Law (Copyright Act 1957) protects original literary, artistic, musical, dramatic works for the lifetime of author + 60 years in India.

---

## 📌 2.8 Domain Names and Issues
├── **Definition:** A Domain Name is a human-readable address (e.g., google.com) mapping to an IP address.
├── **Issues:**
1. **Cybersquatting** – registering trademark domains for resale
2. **Typosquatting** – misspellings to trick users
3. **Domain hijacking** – unauthorized transfer
4. **Trademark conflicts**
5. **Phishing domains**
├── **Resolution:** ICANN UDRP, INDRP for .in
├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Domain names are alphanumeric internet addresses, with key issues including cybersquatting, typosquatting, and trademark conflicts.

---

## 📌 2.9 Patents in Cyber World
├── **Patents in software/cyber world** are controversial:
- US allows software patents (Amazon's 1-click)
- India: Software per se NOT patentable (Sec 3(k))
- Combination with hardware producing technical effect can be patented
- Issues: abstractness, prior art, software vs algorithm

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Patents in the cyber world cover novel hardware-software combinations producing technical effect; pure software/algorithms are not patentable in India.

## 📌 3.5 IT Act 2000 — Sections 46 to 64 and CRAT Rules

### Adjudication (Sec 46-47)
- **Adjudicating Officer** appointed by Central/State Govt (at least Joint Secretary level)
- Power to inquire and adjudicate cases up to **₹5 crore**
- Has powers of civil court

### Cyber Appellate Tribunal (Sec 48-56)
- Now merged with **TDSAT** (since 2017)
- **Composition:** Chairperson + members
- Powers like civil court
- Bound by natural justice, not strict CPC

### Other Procedural Sections
- **Sec 57** – Appeal to High Court within 60 days
- **Sec 58** – Procedure & powers of CRAT
- **Sec 59** – Right to legal representation
- **Sec 60** – Limitation Act applies
- **Sec 61** – Civil court jurisdiction barred
- **Sec 62** – Appeal to High Court
- **Sec 63** – Compounding of contraventions
- **Sec 64** – Recovery of penalty as land revenue arrears

### CRAT Rules:
- Filing procedures, forms (Form A, B), fees
- Service of notice
- Hearing procedures, costs, ex-parte orders
- Modification, review, appeals to HC

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Sections 46-64 establish adjudication mechanism, Cyber Appellate Tribunal (now TDSAT), appeal processes, and procedural rules under IT Act 2000.

# 🟧 UNIT – IV: RIGHTS AND E-GOVERNANCE

## 📌 4.1 Privacy in Cyber World
├── **Definition:** Privacy is the right of an individual to control how their personal information is collected, used, and shared.
├── **Constitutional Right (India):** Recognized as **fundamental right** under Article 21 in **Justice K.S. Puttaswamy vs Union of India (2017)**.
├── **Privacy Issues in Cyber World:**
1. Mass surveillance (govt, NSA)
2. Aadhaar concerns
3. Social media tracking
4. Data brokers selling personal data
5. Cookies and online tracking
6. Location tracking via mobile
7. IoT devices (smart TVs, voice assistants)
8. Biometric data (face recognition)

├── **Indian Laws:**
- **DPDP Act 2023** – Digital Personal Data Protection Act
- **IT Act Sec 43A** – sensitive personal data
- **IT Rules 2011** – SPDI Rules

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Privacy in cyber world is the right to control personal data online, recognized as fundamental right in India and protected by DPDP Act 2023 and IT Act provisions.

├── **Freedom of Speech and Expression** (Article 19(1)(a)):
- Extends to internet
- Limited by reasonable restrictions

4. **Right to Information online**
5. **Online defamation**

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Freedom in cyberspace includes online expression, net neutrality, anonymity, and right to be forgotten — balanced against reasonable restrictions like national security and decency.

## 📌 4.4 International Scenario in Cyber Laws

### A. Data Protection Laws in EU and USA

#### EU – GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation, 2018)
- **Most stringent data protection law globally**
- Applies to EU residents' data, even by non-EU companies
- **Rights granted:**
  - Right to access
  - Right to be forgotten
  - Right to data portability
  - Right to rectification
- **Penalties:** Up to **€20 million or 4% of annual turnover**
- **Principles:** Lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, data minimization

#### USA – Sectoral Approach
- **No comprehensive federal data protection law**
- Sector-specific laws:
  - **HIPAA** – healthcare data
  - **COPPA** – children's privacy
  - **GLBA** – financial info
  - **FERPA** – education records
  - **CCPA / CPRA** – California consumer privacy
- Federal Trade Commission (FTC) enforces

### B. Child Abuse Protection Laws

#### EU
- **EU Child Sexual Abuse Directive (2011/93/EU)**
- Mandates removal/blocking of child sexual abuse material
- Strict penalties

#### USA
- **PROTECT Act of 2003**
- **Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA)**
- **18 U.S. Code § 2251** – child sex exploitation
- Strict prison terms

### C. Cyber Laws – Malaysian Approach
- **Computer Crimes Act 1997** – first cyber law
- **Communications and Multimedia Act 1998**
- **Personal Data Protection Act 2010**
- **Digital Signature Act 1997**
- Targets: unauthorized access, hacking, fraud, content regulation
- Has strong enforcement via MCMC (Malaysian Communications & Multimedia Commission)

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** International cyber laws vary — EU's GDPR is stringent, USA uses sectoral approach (HIPAA, COPPA), Malaysia has comprehensive Computer Crimes Act 1997 and Personal Data Protection Act 2010.

## 📌 5.2 Major Issues in Cyber Evidence Management

### Cyber Evidence:
**Definition:** Any data stored or transmitted electronically that can be used as evidence in legal proceedings.

### Major Issues:
1. **Authenticity** – proving evidence is genuine
2. **Integrity** – ensuring no tampering (use hashes)
3. **Chain of Custody** – documented handling history
4. **Admissibility** – meeting legal standards (Sec 65B Indian Evidence Act)
5. **Volatility** – data lost on power off (RAM)
6. **Volume** – TB-scale data
7. **Encryption** – inaccessible without keys
8. **Preservation** – need for forensic imaging
9. **Cross-border data** – different laws
10. **Skills shortage** – need trained forensic investigators

### Section 65B of Indian Evidence Act
- Specifies conditions for admissibility of electronic evidence
- Requires certificate from custodian
- Ensures genuineness

├── **One-line Exam Answer:** Cyber evidence management involves ensuring authenticity, integrity, chain of custody, and admissibility (under Sec 65B Indian Evidence Act) of digital evidence.
